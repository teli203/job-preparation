Interview

Preparation

1. One Week Before the Interview: Preparation is Key

Research the Company:

Demonstrates your genuine interest in the role and company. Do the research compile your findings and it will show in the interview.

Understand the Job Description:

Helps tailor your answers to align with what the employer is seeking, and what you are capable of bringing to the team.

Know Your Interviewers:

Builds rapport and allows you to anticipate their priorities. To do this requires you to research prior to the interview and to do active listening when the interviewer speaks.

Plan Your Outfit:

Dressing appropriately boosts confidence and shows professionalism. Reseach shows that an individual is more confident when dressed in their best attire. If you have to buy your best attire, try to think of it as an investment in your future self.

2. Three Days Before the Interview: Polish Your Responses

Practice Common Questions:

Prepares you to answer confidently and concisely. You can use note cards, or a word doc, whatever you prefer as long as you prepare for the questions and answers. It helps if you have a friend or family member ask you the questions like a mock interview.

Prepare for Role-Specific Questions:

Shows your expertise and preparedness by knowing the role you applied for. If you are interviewing for a software developer position, be prepared to code and or white board your code out.

Create Your Own Questions:

Demonstrates interest and engagement. Hiring Mangers love a good question from a future employee. So, when asked Do you have any questions for us? Be ready with your questions, and follow ups.

3. The Day Before the Interview: Final Touches

Revisit Key Notes:

Refreshes your memory on critical details. Like the company's mission, values, and the key points from the job description. Reviewing the skills and accomplishments you want to highlight ensures you'll present yourself confidently.

Test Your Tech (for Virtual Interviews):

Ensures a smooth, professional experience. Check your camera, microphone, and internet connection to ensure everything works perfectly. Practice logging into the video platform and adjusting settings for proper lighting and background. This avoids last minute technical hiccups.

Plan Your Route (for In-Person Interviews):

Avoids delays and stress on the interview day. Research the location, figure out the best route, and account for possible delays. Plan to arrive 15-20 minutes early, giving you time to compose yourself before walking in. A stress-free commute helps set a positive tone.

Prepare Materials:

Shows you're organized and prepared. Gather all necessary items: multiple copies of your resume, a list of references, portfolio (if applicable), and a notebook and pen for taking notes. If required, double check that presentations or work samples are organized and ready to present.

4. The Day of the Interview: Show Time

Arrive Early:

Shows punctuality and professionalism. Aim to reach the venue 15 minutes before your scheduled time. For virtual interviews, log in 5-10 minutes early to show you're punctual and prepared. Waiting a little before the meeting starts demonstrates your reliability.

Mind Your Body Language:

Communicates confidence and enthusiasm. Your nonverbal cues communicate volumes. Sit up straight, maintain good eye contact, and avoid crossing your arms or fidgeting. A genuine smile and an attentive posture convey confidence and engagement.

Engage Actively:

Builds a connection with your interviewer. Be an active participant in the conversation. Listen carefully, respond thoughtfully, and tie your answers back to the role and the company's goals. Asking questions during the discussion demonstrates enthusiasm and curiosity.

End on a Strong Note:

Leaves a positive final impression. Finish the interview with a confident close. Reiterate your interest in the position and thank the interviewer for their time. If possible, inquire about the next steps in the hiring process.

5. After the Interview: Follow-Up

Reflect on Your Performance:

Helps you improve for future interviews. Take a moment to evaluate what went well and areas you could improve. Think about how effectively you communicated your skills and how you responded to challenging questions. These insights help you grow for future opportunities.

Stay in Touch:

Keeps you top-of-mind with the employer. If you haven't received a response within the expected timeframe, send a polite follow-up email. Reaffirm your interest and inquire about the status of your application. This keeps you visible without being overbearing.

How:

Send a Thank-You Note (Within 24 Hours):

Politely follow up if you haven't heard back after the stated timeline. Reinforces your interest and professionalism.
